Hey, welcome to my first post. A few weeks ago, I passed the Certified Web Exploitation Specialist (CWES) exam from Hack The Box while simultaneously graduating with my bachelor's degree. As part of my college's enterprise plan, I was able to choose from the various certifications HTB offers, and CWES was the one that stood out to me, as it directly focuses on web exploitation and penetration testing — something I have quite a bit of experience in and genuinely enjoy.

The CWES is a certification offered by HTB that assesses your skills specifically within web application pentesting from a black-box perspective — something you absolutely need to know if you're thinking about becoming a security researcher or getting into bug bounty. It ranks at the same difficulty as the Certified Penetration Testing Specialist (CPTS) exam, which focuses on network penetration testing instead.

To be eligible to take the CWES exam, you must first complete 20 modules within the Web Penetration Tester Job Role Path, which covers server-side attacks, command injection, API attacks, and much more. Since I already had web pentesting experience, the modules weren't too difficult, and they covered the OWASP Top 10 and more very well. Completion time depends on your experience — I've heard many people finish within 3 months, while others took 9 or more. For me, the path was faster since several modules overlapped with my degree curriculum.

### **Is It Worth It?**

Yes! The CWES genuinely covers the majority of what you need to know to get into web application penetration testing or bug bounty. It teaches you everything from a black-box perspective. With organizations rapidly shifting to hybrid and cloud environments, web and API attack surfaces are growing faster than ever. Because of this, I believe the CWES is more valuable than the CPTS, which focuses on traditional network pentesting in a landscape that's becoming increasingly web-first.

### **The Exam**

The exam is 7 days long, and you must solve at least 80% of the flags AND write a fully professional penetration testing report. You're placed into a simulated scenario where you have to test a company's web infrastructure for vulnerabilities — without a single hint or clue. I was able to find all flags within 3 days out of the 7, at roughly 6-8 hours a day.

### **The Report**

The report must meet HTB's guidelines, which you're given once you start the exam. I've seen many people recommend SysReptor for writing the report, but I personally just used Word with the provided template. It's genuinely a professional penetration testing report, so expect to spend a few days on it. For me, it took 2 days.

### **Grading**

After submitting, it took about 3 days for the exam to be graded, and I passed with no issues. From what I've heard, if you don't pass on your first attempt — whether because you didn't find at least 80% of the flags or your report didn't meet the standards — you'll receive feedback on what to fix. You're given two attempts per voucher.

### **Who Should Take It?**

Someone who enjoys web application exploitation and wants to gain advanced knowledge in pentesting with a higher chance of success in bug bounty. Who shouldn't? Someone who doesn't take the modules seriously, is interested in network pentesting, or wants an easy cert.

### **Tips for the Exam**

1. Seriously pay attention to the modules. The exam covers exactly what's taught — you won't need outside resources. Study them extensively and reference the techniques during the exam.
2. Practice on the module assessments. They're essentially mini versions of the real exam, so try to master them.
3. Take screenshots and notes during the exam. I chose to write my report after finishing all flags, and I was only able to do so because I documented every step along the way — critical for the final report.
4. Build your own methodology. I used a structured methodology to systematically test everything possible. If you go in without one, you'll struggle.

As for me, the CWEE is the natural next step after CWES, and something I plan on taking in the future, as it focuses on web application testing from a white-box perspective.
